Comparing the quality of various news sources using OpenAI

  • 29 December 2024
  • OpenAI,
  • Gemini

I am often shocked by how misleading and manipulative some online media platforms can be. Yet, they still attract a significant number of readers, many of whom view the sources I trust with similar skepticism. This made me curious about gaining an "independent" perspective on which news outlets are more reliable. To explore this, I decided to create a simple tool that analyzes articles from various sources and uses OpenAI to assign a score to each. It will be fascinating to see if these scores reveal noticeable differences between the sites.

What to evaluate

I defined four attributes for which OpenAI will assign a score between 0 and 10:

  • Credibility: Higher is better, indicating how believable the article's claims are.
  • Mood: Reflects the emotional tone of the article, ranging from sad to happy.
  • Manipulativeness: Lower is better, measuring how manipulative the article's content appears.
  • Complexity: Evaluates the complexity of the language and wording used in the article.

I collected articles over the course of 2 months, but including the full content of each would have been too costly for this project. Instead, I opted to analyze only the titles and subtitles of the articles.

How I collected the data

Instead of scraping, I subscribed to the RSS feeds of four Hungarian news sites—444, Telex, Origo, and Magyar Nemzet—and extracted the title and description of each article. For simplicity, I implemented this logic in a GitHub Action that ran several times a day. Each time, it used OpenAI to analyze the articles and then committed the raw data to the repository. While this setup is not scalable, my goal was simply to create a functional prototype quickly.

I used GPT-3.5-turbo for the analysis. For comparison, I also evaluated the scores using Gemini 1.5-Flash, which produced nearly identical results.

Results

I collected two months' worth of data this summer, but unfortunately, the results weren't as exciting as I had hoped. In practice, there was little to no difference between the four news sites in terms of these scores. However, there are still a few interesting observations regarding the general attributes of these news outlets.

I believe that using a more powerful model or analyzing English sources could improve the scores. It's likely that these models perform weaker when processing Hungarian language content.

1. There is generally a bad mood of political articles

2. The articles seem generally believable to AI

3. Most articles seem manipulative to AI

However, it seems to contradict its judgment regarding credibility, which makes me suspect that some of these estimations might be inaccurate. Reworking the prompt or using a stronger model could likely help address this issue.
